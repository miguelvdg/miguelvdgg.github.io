Questioning Google’s Search Engine Dominance¶

Guy Krekelberg, Miguel van de Goor and Joost Melchers, January 2016

In our notebook we will illustrate how the market structure develops in a highly concentrated search engine market and whether a higher concentration will lead to diminishing welfare or not. By applying a contestable market model of Argenton & Prfer (2012) we will be able to analytically and graphically show the different market structure developments and benefits.

This notebook page is mainly for educational purposes and could be referred to for policy analysis.

Research Question.¶

How does welfare develop over time in differently structured search engine markets?

Sub Question.¶

Does imposing perfect competition as a policy, concerning complete transparency of the installed database, improve innovation, search quality, consumer surplus, and total welfare?

Motivation.¶

We all use it on a daily basis: a search engine. We rely on it. Our guess is that you make your search queries with Google, just like a majority of the world population. Googles global market share for desktop use has been rising from 68 until 78 percent in 2016 alone, and in the mobile and tablet segment it has remained around a staggering 94 percent. These numbers tell us that Google has retrieved considerable market power and is close to monopolization. It is therefore not a surprise that European Commission has been investigating the tech giant. In a press release of July 2016 two statements have come forward:

Google has abused its dominant position by systematically favouring its comparison shopping service in its search result pages and the company has abused its dominant position by artificially restricting the possibility of third party websites to display search advertisements from Google's competitors.

These results have been supported by Patterson (2013) as well. Furthermore, Pollock (2013) also shows that the welfare-optimizing search quality deviates from that of the profit-maximizing search quality. However, in light of these statements, is market dominance automatically a bad thing? It could it be a reflection of delivering higher search quality, in which case a monopoly is a reward to its honest efforts. Antitrust prosecutions would herewith disincentive superiority. But, as Peter Norvig, Googles leading research guru, once put it: Google doesnt have better algorithms it just has more data. Being superior in search quality with increasing complexity algorithms is nowadays not the reason for market dominance. Therefore the statements by the EU of Google abusing its market power can hardly be defended.

As you may have noticed, Googles market power is under scrutiny and should be questioned. It is therefore of interest to see how it has developed until this point over time for it to make it understandable, but also to see how policies could influence market competition in benefit of consumers and total welfare.

Method.¶

In order to show that the development in the search engine market is worrying, we solve the Argenton & Prfer model for each possible competition level inside the market. The three levels we consider are the competitive triopoly, the non-competitive triopoly and the duopoly. Market shares, profits, producer surplus, consumer surplus and total welfare are computed. Then we will show which policy needs to be implemented in order to make the search engine market healthy again, and compare this possible policy implementation to the current situation. A reality check is done in order to prove how the market has been developing over time. At last, a senstivity analysis will be executed to show the effect of an increase in advertising revenue on the market conditions.

Main Assumptions.¶

  1. The advertising revenue of a search engine is proportional to its market share, which is broadly in line with industrys figures. This is also supported with advertisers reasoning where the amount of visitors increases its demand.
  2. Consumers can only rationally expect search engine quality to be different on average, for they do not know which engine delivers the best quality for each query. Therefore the firm's market share is monotonically increasing in its perceived average search quality.
  3. There is a unit mass of consumers, each of which has demand for one query, and the cost for a consumers to use a search engine is zero.
  4. Moreover, the quality is high enough such that everyone will use a search engine, meaning that the market is fully covered.

Introducing Model Characteristics¶

$x_i$ = the simultaneous search quality of firm $i$

$N_i$ = the installed base of firm $i$, in other words it is the amound of previous search queries run

$F$ = the fixed cost for operations

$p$ = the exogenously given advertising revenue associated to one consumer

$D_i = \frac{x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}$ = the market share of firm $i$

$C(x_i) = \frac{x_i}{N_i}$ = the costs of production of quality $x_i$

The maximization problem each firm will solve is the following:

$\underset{x_i}{\text{max}} \hspace{10pt} \Pi_i = \frac{x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}p - \frac{x_i}{N_i} - F$

The Triopoly Case¶

Since the search engine market is a triopoly at first, where the model is a static game with complete information, the (Nash) Equilibrium values are obtained by solving the set of reaction functions $x_i(p, N, \sum{x_j})$. Where the equilibrium values $x_i^*$ are as follows:

$ \begin{align} x_1 & = \sqrt{pN_1(x_2 + x_3)} - x_2 - x_3 && x_2 = \sqrt{pN_2(x_1 + x_3)} - x_1 - x_3 &&& x_3 = \sqrt{p(x_1 + x_2)} - x_1 - x_2 \\ x_1^* & = \sqrt{\frac{p^2N^2_1N^2_2}{(N_2+N_1(1+N_2))^2}} - \frac{4pN_1N^2_2}{(N_2+N_1(1+N_2))^2} && x_2^* = \frac{2pN_1N_2(N_1(N_2-1)+N_2)}{(N_2+N_1(1+N_2))^2} &&& x_3^* = \frac{2pN_1N_2(-N_1(N_2-1)+N_2)}{(N_2+N_1(1+N_2))^2}\\ \end{align} $

The python code to show the development of $x_i^*$ over $N_1$ is plotted below, where $N_2$ is taken as a constant ($N_3$ is irrelevant for the equilibrium), $p$ is set to 1 and fixed costs are considered nil:

As we can see, a clear divergence happens as the installed base of firm 1 $N_1$ increases. The search quality $x_1$ increases due to more query logs in their search engine and the competitors' quality suffer from their loss in search queries run on their engine. So, that $x_2$ and $x_3$ both decrease, but since firm 3 started with the lowest quality they are hit the most and will leave the market when $N_1$ gets big enough and their own quality diminishes.

The market shares $D_i^* = \frac{x_i^*}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^*}$ develop the same way as $x_i^*$, since recall that the market share is monotonically increasing in its perceived average search quality. The Nash equilibrium market shares are:

$ \begin{align} & D_1^* = 1 - \frac{2N_2}{N_2 + N_1(1+N_2)} && D_2^* = 1 - \frac{2N_1}{N_2 + N_1(1+N_2)} &&& D_3^* = \frac{N_1(1-N_2)+N_2}{N_2+N_1(1+N_2)} \end{align} $

Just like in the previous two graphs, we notice the same divergence, but then in profits. Firm 1 clearly benefits from their market share increase and firm 2 and 3 suffer. Yet again, firm 2 manages to keep making a small amount of profits.

However, to see whether the modelled development in a triopoly case benefits society, besides only firms' profits, we will have a look at welfare and distinguish between producer and consumer surplus, where welfare = producer surplus + consumer surplus:

$ Producer \, Surplus = \Pi_1 + \Pi_2 + \Pi_3 = \dfrac{(3N_2^2 - 2N_1N_2(1+N_2)+N_1^2(3+N_2(3N_2-2)))}{N_2+N_1(1+N_2))^2}p-3F $

$ Consumer \, Surplus = D_1x_1^*+D_2x_2^*+D_3x_3^* = \dfrac{2N_1N_2(3N_2^2-2N_1N_2(1+N_2)+N_1^2(3+N_2(3N_2-2))}{N_2+N_1(1+N_2))^3}p $

$ Welfare = PS + CS = \dfrac{(N_2+N_1(3N_2+1))(3N_2^2-2N_1N_2(1+N_2)+N_1^2(3+N_2(3N_2-2))}{N_2+N_1(1+N_2))^3}p-3F $

The Duopoly Case¶

Over time in the triopoly case, a firm will not be able to compete anymore and make profits. In that instance he will leave the market and a duopoly scenario exists. In this part we will graphically show what happens in the duopoly case. The model assumptions, characteristics and profit maximization problem remain untouched, however there will only be two reaction function to solve for the Nash Equilibrium $x_i^*$ and $D_I^*$:

$ \begin{align} & x_1^* = \frac{N_1^2}{(1+N_1)^2} && x_2^* = \frac{N_1}{(1+N_1)^2} \\ & D_1^* = \frac{N_1}{1+N_1} && D_2^* = \frac{1}{1+N_1} \end{align} $

Policy Recommendation¶

In contrast to the previous cases, in which $N_i \neq N_j$, the policy which is recommended is that the query logs amongst all search engines are shared such that $N_1 = N_j$. That is, the installed bases are equal and we have $N = N_1 + N_2 + N_3$. This situation is dubbed a Competitive Triopoly, since the tipping of the market is not due to the differences in installed bases anymore. The market has become competitive in $x_i$ only.

The Nash Equilibrium values for the quality level is: $x_1^* = x_2^* = x_3^* = \tfrac{2}{9}Np = x^{competitive}$. Where this results in equal market share of one-third for each. The equilibrium profits, consumer surplus, producer surplus and welfare are given as:

$\Pi_1 = \Pi_2 = \Pi_3 = \tfrac{p}{9}-F $

$Producer \, Surplus = \tfrac{p}{3} - 3F $

$Consumer \, Surplus = \tfrac{2}{9}Np $

$and \, Welfare = \tfrac{p}{9}(3+2N)-3F $

def producersurplus_triopoly(N_1,N_2,p,F):
    return ((3*N_2-2*N_1*N_2*(1+N_2)+N_1**2*(3+N_2*(3*N_2-2)))*p/(N_2+N_1*(1+N_2))**2-3*F)

def consumersurplus_triopoly(N_1,N_2,p):
    return (2*N_1*N_2*(3*N_2**2-2*N_1*N_2*(1+N_2)+N_1**2*(3+N_2*(3*N_2-2))))*p/(N_2+N_1*(1+N_2))**3

def welfare_triopoly(N_1,N_2,p,F):
    return (((N_2+N_1*(3*N_2+1))*(3*(N_2**2)-2*N_1*N_2*(1+N_2)+(N_1**2)*(3+N_2*(3*N_2-2)))*p)/(N_2+N_1*(1+N_2))**3)-3*F
